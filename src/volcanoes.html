Ring of Fire


The Ring of Fire is a region around the rim of the Pacific Ocean where many volcanic eruptions occur. It is caused due to the movement and collision of lithospheric plates (the Pacific Plate). Most of Earth's active volcanoes are located in the Ring of Fire and many of them are stratovolcanoes.









Different volcano types are formed based on certain properties of the magma:

  • the viscosity, or stickiness, of the magma
  • the amount of gas in the magma
  • the composition of the magma
  • the way in which the magma reached the surface



    • Stratovolcanoes


    Stratovolcanoes (or composite volcano) have steep sides and are conical with many layers. Their lava is extremely viscous, i.e it does not flow easily. The lava builds up around the vent of the volcano, which leads to steep sides. Stratovolcanoes often have explosive and effusive eruptions because of gas build up in the viscous lava. Stratovolcanoes are mostly found along plate tectonic boundaries (oceanic crust is under continental crust). Eg: Japan, Philippines, Aleutian Islands.



    Calderas


    Large eruptions that empty the magma chamber can cause the roof of the magma chamber to collapse and form a depression. This is called a caldera. Calderas can be formed from explosive eruptions that can remove the summit of a stratovolcano. Eg: Crater Lake, USA



    Shield


    Shield volcanoes have low viscosity lava (i.e it spreads easily). Because the lava spreads it forms a volcano with gentle slopes. Repeated eruptions lead to the accumulation of lava, building up the volcano. Shield volcanoes are some of the biggest in the world. Eg: Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa, Hawaii, USA
